Aid worker diary: Witnessing people’s strength in a crisis

3 Oct 2014

September 2014: Evacuation centers have been set up as alert grows around the eruption of the Mayon Volcano. Credit: OCHA/Tristan Arao
When a disaster strikes in the Philippines, OCHA’s Agnes Placio is one of the first responders to arrive. Agnes recently visited the Mayon volcano, which is threatening to erupt. Here is her report.

When a disaster strikes in the Philippines, OCHA’s Agnes Placio is one of the first responders to arrive. Agnes, a Filipino, understands the challenges faced by her people, and she uses her knowledge of her country to help OCHA Philippines respond. Agnes recently visited the Mayon volcano, which is threatening to erupt. Here is her report.

My country, the Philippines, is part of the Pacific Ring of Fire and the third most-disaster-prone country in the world. Its people are resilient beyond belief. I am very proud to be Filipino and incredibly privileged to bear witness to their strength and irrepressible spirit when they are hit by crisis after crisis.

The most recent threat to Filipinos is in Albay Province in the Bicol region—my parents’ birthplace and an area very close to my heart. Alert levels for the Mayon volcano rose at the beginning of August when the Philippine Institute of Volcanology and Seismology (PHIVOLCS) announced an increase in activity, bringing it to Alert Level 1 (low-level unrest). On 15 August it jumped to Alert Level 2 (moderate unrest), and since mid-September rumblings from the volcano increased. When Alert Level 3 was announced, people in the danger zone were evacuated to safety.

The people who live in the shadow of Mayon accepted their plight long ago. Living so close to a volcano is not easy. I have vivid memories of the eruption in 2009. Mayon began misbehaving, and people were moved to evacuation centres where they waited for three long months before the volcano finally erupted. It’s an anxious time for everybody. Nature decides what happens, and in the meantime some 50,500 volcano neighbours are at the mercy of nature. They wait, uncertain when they can return home, while farms, livestock and livelihoods are affected. 

When members of the Government invited the Humanitarian Country Team to join them on a recent assessment, I was happy to represent OCHA. Typhoon Rammasun (locally known as Glenda) came crashing through the same area in July and wreaked havoc. Prior to the typhoon, the local government unit of Albay Province implemented a pre-evacuation plan, and there were no casualties as a result. The “zero casualty” battle cry of the Albay Province management council certainly succeeded on that occasion, and they are determined to maintain that policy.

When the Mayon volcano blows ash, it causes flight delays in the area. So we decided to travel by road from Manila to the province to conduct the assessment. With a 10-hour drive ahead of us, we departed well before the sun rose at 3 a.m. My hunch to travel by road proved well founded: that day’s flights were cancelled and other members of the assessment team had to travel in a Government C180 aircraft.

The local schools are used as evacuation centres, and many of the most vulnerable people, the young and the elderly are taking refuge there. The men travel each day to tend to their animals and farms, growing rice and vegetables. Those who stay in the evacuation centres feel safer. In the still of the night, the rumbling of the volcano seems louder and more threatening. I spoke to people who fear that it will erupt at any time.

Many children still in education now live in the schools. The Government requested tent schools from UNICEF, and so far it has received 150 tents so that children’s education can continue. Sanitation facilities are required with latrines to cater for the increased numbers of people using the schools. Dust masks are also required to prevent respiratory infections, which can be fatal when volcanic ash falls.

We returned to the office in Manila after the assessment. For the people in the evacuation centres, it’s now a waiting game: waiting for the volcano to erupt, waiting to go home, waiting to continue with their lives. Their safety is a priority for the Government and the humanitarian community. But the volcanic eruption will be the final scene in this drama, allowing people to  return to the foot of the mountain and continue with their lives.
