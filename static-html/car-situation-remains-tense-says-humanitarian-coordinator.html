CAR: situation remains tense, says Humanitarian Coordinator

19 Oct 2015

Aurélien Agbénonci, Humanitarian Coordinator in the Central African Republic visits an IDP camp in Kaga Bandoro. Credit: Nektarios Markogiannis/MINUSCA
The latest crisis in the country was sparked by the killing of a young man on 26 September. His death ignited intercommunity violence that left at least 77 people dead, 414 injured, and 24,200 newly displaced.

Aurélien Agbénonci has been the Humanitarian Coordinator in the Central African Republic (CAR) since May 2015. He talked to us about the current security and humanitarian situation in CAR. The latest crisis in the country was sparked by the killing of a young Muslim man on 26 September. His death ignited intercommunity violence that left at least 77 people dead, 414 seriously injured, 23,239 displaced and forced 1,086 people to the Democratic Republic of the Congo.

Please describe the current security situation in Bangui and beyond.

The security situation in Bangui is calm now but still highly volatile. The recent violence drove up tensions leading to an increase in criminal activities, including the setting up of [illegal] barricades on main roads and the looting and burning of houses. Economic activities are slowly normalizing and commercial flights have resumed, although public administration remains partially closed.

I remain very concerned about the adverse effect this crisis is having outside Bangui in the provinces, where a rise in tensions among armed groups continue to lead to clashes. Kemo province was the most affected region, in the town of Dekoa, where 10,000 people were freshly displaced.

What are the humanitarian priorities?

The latest fighting has worsened an already dire humanitarian situation, limiting humanitarian access countrywide and impeding affected people’s access to schools and hospitals.

In spite of these access constraints, humanitarian agencies are providing assistance wherever they are able to.
Medical teams in Bangui are working relentlessly—even risking their lives—to provide emergency health care for injured people, rape survivors, pregnant women and newborns. I strongly commend their efforts and bravery in the fight to save lives and alleviate suffering. The World Food Programme and NGOs have been distributing food aid in 12 IDP sites in Bangui since 7 October. Nutrition assistance is also being provided to malnourished children, and a radio awareness-raising programme was launched to encourage people who had abandoned treatment to return to nutritional management units.

Some health facilities remained operational even during the crisis, but access was impeded in most areas. The health facility in the Castor neighbourhood, for example, went from delivering 30 babies per day prior to the crisis to zero. People require food, shelter, protection, drinking water, sanitation, and access to basic social services including schools and hospitals.

A hotline (4040) managed by the Danish Refugee Council received over 700 calls between 26 September and 4 October. Most of the calls were seeking help to report population movements and cases of insecurity, including looting and the destruction of religious buildings. Some callers also sought protection advice, such as medical referrals or legal advice.

In a nutshell, the situation is extremely challenging, but we are equal to the task at hand. The Central African people need us now and we will not fail them.

How are humanitarian workers coping?

For the first time since the crisis in CAR started in December 2013, humanitarian workers have been directly targeted. The offices and residences of national and international NGOs were looted, obliging about 300 humanitarian staff to be temporarily relocated outside of the country. These direct attacks did not only put staff lives in danger but it also reduced NGOs’ operational capacity. I strongly condemn this renewed violence against the civilian population in Bangui and call on all parties to respect international humanitarian law.

The rest of the humanitarian community in the Central African Republic is committed to staying and delivering assistance. Although the security situation has deteriorated, we must continue with the emergency response. The needs of displaced persons are increasing every day and our main aim is to help them.

How is the humanitarian community collaborating with MINUSCA (the Multidimensional Integrated Stabilization Mission in the Central African Republic) without jeopardizing humanitarian space?

In our specific roles and responsibilities, we must never forget why we are here: first and foremost, both entities have a common objective to serve the Central African people. Military escorts were requested from the peacekeeping force as a last resort and we certainly had no option, faced with imminent danger. We also sought military escorts to transport a stock of medical supplies that were at risk of being looted and were worth US$500,000, to and from health centres. We are coordinating with MINUSCA to ensure people most at risk of attack are protected.

What is the impact of the situation on children?

Children were deliberately targeted in the latest flare of violence. Preliminary reports from local organizations indicate that three boys aged between 16 and 17 were brutally killed, including one who was decapitated, while at least two boys and two girls were wounded by gunshots or grenade fragments in brutal clashes between armed groups.

This latest escalation in violence is likely to further deteriorate conditions for children, many of whom are already suffering from disrupted health services, critical levels of child malnutrition, closed schools and forced recruitment by armed groups. Although the school year began on 21 September, most schools remain closed due to the violence. Insecurity on the road between Cameroon and CAR is causing a major delay in the delivery of educational and recreational supplies. UNICEF and NGOs are providing safe spaces for children to learn and play and psychological support for the most affected. They are also negotiating with armed groups to try to ensure they [the children] are released from armed forces and reintegrated with communities. Since May this year, 749 children have been liberated from armed groups in CAR.

Why is violence surging again, and what needs to be done to tackle its root causes to stop this from happening again?

I am strongly convinced that the crisis in this country is fuelled by poverty. The Central African Republic is 185 out of 187 countries ranked on the humanitarian development index, which is even lower than in 2011. Over the past few years, the collapse of the economic fabric marked by the lack of investment, the reduction in trade and the closing of several job-generating companies all led to a drop in household income, increasing the poverty rate. Such a situation of massive unemployment makes the youth an easy target to be manipulated and recruited into armed groups.
Other factors that help fuel religious, intercommunity tensions include bad governance and the proliferation of light weapons due to the porosity of borders, among many others.

In order to address the root causes of the violence and to break this vicious cycle, I remain adamant that the root causes of poverty must be addressed. A more stable political atmosphere is fundamental to restoring State authority and the relaunch of socioeconomic activities. Hopefully the upcoming presidential and legislative elections scheduled before the end of the year, will enable the country to break out of this impasse into the road to recovery. Most importantly, continuous efforts must be made to build trust between communities in the country. It will not be easy, but this is the only way forward.
