Daily Brief: Deteriorating security in North Kivu, humanitarian update on Mali

22 Jan 2016

May 2015, North Kivu, DRC. Credit: OCHA/Nadia Berger
The security situation in DR Congo continues to deteriorate, delaying the delivery of aid and other essential operations. Toby Lanzer visited Mali where he saw first hand progress made and persisting challenges.

DR Congo

  • The Head of OCHA in the Democratic Republic of Congo, Rein Paulsen, today highlighted the deteriorating security in North Kivu. For months now, the security situation has been deteriorating. In Beni, Lubero, Masisi, Rutshuru and Walikale territories, kidnappings of aid workers and civilians and attacks on convoys have increased, often forcing organizations to delay the delivery of aid and other essential operations or suspend activities entirely.
  • On 20 January Médecins Sans Frontières announced the closure of its medical project in Mweso, Masisi territory after one of its convoys was robbed and two staff abducted in December. The Mweso project provided medical humanitarian assistance to close to 200,000 patients in 2015 and expected to do the same, if not more, in 2016.
  • “Delivering life-saving aid and protecting civilians in North Kivu, one of the most volatile provinces in the country, has become a herculean task in recent weeks due to kidnappings and increased insecurity, further restricting access. When major, seasoned humanitarian organizations such as MSF decide to leave an area for security reasons, it means the situation is of grave concern."
  • Mr. Paulsen also noted that the ambient insecurity makes it much harder for the population to access essential humanitarian aid, and means that many organizations have constrained or suspended humanitarian activities.
  • “Without urgent action to increase the protection of aid workers, we could be collectively failing to protect the very people we are here to serve. OCHA and partners call on all Congolese authorities and all relevant stakeholders to do even more to improve the security situation allowing humanitarian organizations to work and save lives, and civilians can live in security."
  • More than 7.5 million people need humanitarian aid across the country. 

Mali

  • The Regional Humanitarian Coordinator for the Sahel, Toby Lanzer, concluded a week long mission to Mali today where he witnessed progress made and persisting challenges regarding security and access to basic services for communities affected by the crisis.
  • While important progress has been made - schools have reopened and access to safe water and essential healthcare has improved, Mr Lanzer noted that, “The continued redeployment of State authorities is essential if we are to consolidate existing gains and strengthen the much needed recovery and resilience work with the support of humanitarian and development partners. "The youth, women and community leaders I met, all have a vision for the future of their society; and they want to be part of its shaping. Mali is at a turning point in its history and the authorities must remain fully committed to seizing this opportunity. At the same time, the international community must maintain and scale up its support to enable people to realize their expectation for peace, justice and development."
  • Significant needs remain, notably to ensure access to water in rural areas and Kidal, and avoid a deterioration of public health and livestock’s conditions. Support for the next planting season is also essential to safeguard the food security of households.
  • Mbaranga Gasarabwe, the Humanitarian Coordinator for Mali, who just visited areas in northern Mali, said that the signing of the Peace Agreement and its implementation by all parties should allow for broader humanitarian access to meet urgent needs, and strengthen communities’ self-reliance. “During the coming months, expected improvements in the security situation will be essential to ensure populations’ access to basic services."  
  • The humanitarian response plan for Mali calls for US$ 354 million this year.
