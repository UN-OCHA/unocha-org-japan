Nepal: three months on, volunteers show no sign of weariness

24 Jul 2015

Pabitrya Paudyal, 13, reads a book that she found among the rubble of destroyed Chaturmala Higher Secondary School in Muchowk, Gorkha, Nepal. Photo: UNICEF/Panday/May 2015
When the earthquake hit Nepal on 25 April, the first responders were community members themselves. Three months on, these volunteers remain at the forefront of the response.

When the earthquake hit Nepal on 25 April, the first responders were community members themselves: neighbours, family-members and friends grouping around to help one another. When rescuers and aid workers started to arrive to help, many community members joined the relief effort, most as unpaid volunteers. 

Three months on, these volunteers remain at the forefront of the response. Their roles have shifted from assessing needs, delivering food and other life-saving supplies, to helping build temporary shelters and other activities. These volunteers have shown commitment, dedication and persistence, many of them putting their own lives and jobs on hold to help the affected, even when their own houses were destroyed.

“It gives you a special feeling when you carry a wounded person to the hospital for treatment, or are able to bring a smile to the face of someone crying,” said Baburam Dankuti, 31, from Gorkha district, the epicenter of the first quake. Hailing from the so-called “Dalit” or ing from the community, Baburam is one of thousands of volunteers who immediately set out to help others in need despite their own house being decimated.

Jamie McGoldrick, the Humanitarian Coordinator in Nepal, was once a UN volunteer himself. He recently told a group of volunteers: “As a former UN volunteer, I can say this is a once in a lifetime opportunity for you to use your skill to help your community and your country.” 

To this day, volunteers are continuing to serve the broader community, even though they themselves have experienced intense losses in the earthquake. OCHA spoke with volunteers in Kathmandu and in affected villages. Meet some of them: 

The Scholar: fundraising and coordination

Many Nepalese who come from the country’s southern plains – they are known as ‘Madhesis’ – rushed back to their homes after the earthquake. But Sujeet Karn, a visiting Social Work professor at Nepal’s Tribhuvan University in Kathmandu, did the opposite, leaving his wife and their four-week-old baby, to head for Nuwakot in the north-west of the capital, one of the 14 districts that had been hit hard.

“By the third day, I had visited most of the places in Kathmandu,” said Sujeet, who witnessed victims being pulled out from rubble in the capital’s Balaju area. “I saw many people out in the field and thought there was no dearth of volunteers in Kathmandu. That’s why I set out for Nuwakot with noodles, oral rehydration solution, water purifier and rice.”

Because of his ethnicity, Sujeet Karn said: “some villagers asked me if I was from India, but it was not of much concern.” As a PhD on Social Sciences from the University of Hull in the United Kingdom, he used his international network to generate money for his humanitarian mission and mobilized 24 of his own students to help deliver the aid.

“There was opposition from my family initially, but our house was intact and that spurred my decision to get out and help those in need,” he told IRIN. His wife also helped out, using social media to spread the word and gather more supplies. In the end they raised US$8000 in cash, which he used to build 40 shacks, to buy and hand out 1,000 blankets, and distribute food to 350 families in Nuwakot.

He also partnered with a doctors’ association in India to organize 18 health camps in Nuwakot, Kathmandu and Kavre districts, and separately distributed more than 500 solar lights to schoolchildren.

This highly organized response was informed by Sujeet's previous experience responding to the 2001 earthquake that hit India’s Gujarat region when he was a student in Mumbai.

The Artists: generating hope through murals

Unfazed by the dust, the constant honking of vehicles on the road just a few metres away, and the scorching pre-monsoon sun, a group of young artists gathered in Kathmandu in mid-June, to paint a mural on the side wall of a busy street in the capital’s Babarmahal neighbourhood.

The mural depicted the Kasthamandap temple, the 16th century Kathmandu monument flattened by the devastating 25 April quake. Their aim: to trigger “a new revolution” of development and reconstruction.

“This disaster can be taken as both a challenge and an opportunity,” said the coordinator of the mural project, Bhishan Rajbhandari, as he took break from putting finishing touches to the mural, which measures 19 by 6 metres.

“We made this [mural] so that the people will not forget the temple. Whoever sees this will develop the feeling that this should be rebuilt,” said Bhishan. “It is also a small gift from artists to the people of Nepal.”

It took 16 days for more than 30 young artists to complete the artwork, which is the first among nine such projects. Bhishan said his team of young artists has already prepared the 3D designs of eight other temples – all of them destroyed in the quake.

Artist Hari Om Mehta, 29, said through their work the youth are trying to put pressure on the government to rebuild the destroyed heritage sites sooner than later.

Like thousands of other volunteers, these young artists helped deliver aid in the immediate aftermath of the quake. Later, they realized that life's basics were not enough to heal the wounds of the people. “This can also be considered as art therapy,” said artist and volunteer Santosh Sigdel, 30. “People passing by stop for once and look at it before moving on.”

Speaking at the official opening of the mural, on 22 June, the Chief Secretary of the Government of Nepal, Leela Mani Paudyal, said of the volunteers’ initiative: “Your work generated hope. Your spontaneous and voluntary work links societies. Your work gives me the feeling that we can rebuild this nation.”

The Doctor: connecting the dots

Physician and former surgeon Dr. Bishal Dhakal sprang into action within hours of the 25 April quake, becoming the first Nepali doctor to reach the Barpak village in Gorkha, the epicentre of the quake. Emergency response was in his blood: he had grown up in disaster situations with his father who was a Red Cross worker.

“When I was a small kid I would to go to Red Cross gatherings to areas affected by flooding to distribute kits,” said Bishal, speaking in his office. “If I take my kid [to witness these situations] from now on, he will be a socially conscious animal. I grew up in that culture where humanitarian service was fabled.”

By the second day after the quake, he had 10 volunteers, five days on he had 2,000. “This was a “spontaneous integration of young people for venting in a social grief,” said the doctor.

“You can solve this problem by bringing in spontaneous volunteers and the supply chains. For me, citizenship is playing your role when needed.” Collecting money was not Bishal’s forte so he enlisted some business friends to do so. Luckily, one of them was with the Federation of Nepalese Chambers of Commerce, raising US$50,000 in seed money.

While Bishal continued to provide aid and medical assistance to affected communities, his business friends helped raise an amazing US$1.6 million worth of goods and services. “That is civilian volunteerism,” he explained. “It’s about how you connect the dots.”

The Engineers: building shelters

Engineering graduates fresh out of school, 23-year-old Prateek Pokharel and 24-year-old Bhuwan Gurung thought the 25 April quake offered them an opportunity to finally apply their knowledge and skills. They set about constructing semi-permanent shelters in the Kathmandu and Dolakha districts.

“We thought if we do this, we could preserve the originality of buildings and empower resilience of the locals,” said Prateek, as he showed to UN-OCHA the first shelter they built in Bungmati, in the southern edge of the capital Kathmandu.

Started with seven of their colleagues with the motto “WE CAN,” they built shelters using locally available materials such as wood, bamboo and corrugated galvanized iron sheets that were retrieved from the destroyed houses. The only imported materials they used were the wires for reinforcement.

“We were worried about the resettlement of the victims in the face of the looming monsoons,” said Bhuwan. “We tried to build the shelters by making optimum use of local materials, human resources and techniques; we tried to build a new house using the debris from the old house.” In the end, each shelter costs US$150 to rebuild.

Surya Lal Tuladhar, a wood sculptor who carves statues of the Hindu God Krishna, was one of the group’s beneficiaries. He now shares the shelter with nine other family members and relatives.

“This is good even during the monsoon. We already tested it,” said a beaming Surya, who has been busy planting rice since the onset of this year’s monsoon.

“The 12 May quake [the most powerful aftershock following the first jolt] did not even shake the replica of the Krishna temple,” he said, pointing to the replica made by his wife and put atop the door. “I will build a bigger house after earning money.”
