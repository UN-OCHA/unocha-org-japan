Niger and Chad: “A fragile island of stability in a region of conflict,” says OCHA Operations Director

6 May 2015

28 April 2015, Baga-Sola, Chad: OCHA's Director of Operations John Ging discusses the situation of Chadian IDPs in the Lac region. Their needs are particularly critical in terms of food security, shelter, health, nutrition and education. Credit: OCHA/Mayanne MUNAN
"Niger and Chad making heroic efforts to cope with the impact of turmoil in the region", says John Ging following recent trip to the Sahel.

OCHA Director of Operations, John Ging, spoke about his recent trip to Niger and Chad in the Sahel region at Tuesday’s press briefing at the UN Headquarters in New York, drawing attention to the extent of humanitarian need in two countries hit by conflict-induced displacement, pockets of chronic drought, widespread food insecurity, and structural vulnerability.

Both countries are currently making heroic efforts to cope with the impact of the violence in Nigeria and Central African Republic by hosting more than 850,000 refugees and returnees, while facing down the threat of cross-border attacks. Both countries are participating in a Multi-National Joint Task Force which will take a regional approach to fighting Boko Haram.

“These two countries constitute a fragile island of stability in a region of conflict,” said Mr. Ging. “Impoverished as they are, they show us their exemplary humanity in opening their borders to hundreds of thousands of refugees fleeing their war-torn neighbours.”

Niger, with its population of 18 million, ranks last on the Human Development Index, while Chad falls fourth from last. According to latest figures, 2.4 million people in Chad and 2.6 million in Niger are food insecure.

The international community, however, is failing in its responsibility to share the burden, stressed Ging. Both countries’ humanitarian appeals are severely underfunded, at just 17 per cent for Chad and 25 per cent for Niger.

“It is shameful that we as the international community are not doing more to shoulder our part of this responsibility,” said Mr. Ging. “In a world in crisis, it is imperative that we do not allow these two countries to be forgotten.”

Humanitarian conditions across the Sahel region remain fragile, as many countries grapple with ongoing violence and conflict, erratic weather patterns and chronic drought, acute poverty, and minimal infrastructure.

While immediate lifesaving assistance continues to help some of those in desperate need, Mr. Ging urged the international community to do more to support host countries to build a more hopeful future for their people by investing in education, job creation and public infrastructure. “This is important not only for the people of Niger and Chad, but for the long-term stability of this troubled region,” he said.
