Shaken but not broken: Women recover from abuse

1 Sep 2015

Women celebrate International Women's Day in Kaga-Bandoro. Credit: IRC/Christian Vovi
In CAR, an estimated 47 per cent of women have experienced sexual assault by age 15. 65 per cent of women and men have experienced intimate partner violence. But without adequate funding, aid groups struggle to provide assistance to survivors.

Gender-based violence is widespread in the Central African Republic (CAR) and the statistics are alarming: an estimated 47 per cent of women have experienced sexual assault by age 15 and 65 per cent of women and men have experienced intimate partner violence according to a study conducted by the Ministry of Social Affairs, National Solidarity and Gender. Since the conflict that broke out in CAR in 2012 the GBV sub-cluster received regular reports of “new” GBV incidents.

There are reports of rape, sexual slavery and kidnapping for forced marriage committed by parties to conflict. As UNFPA’s GBV Coordinator, Noemi Dalmonte put it: “Sexual violence is a social and conflict-related transmitted disease in CAR”.

In CAR, an average of 60 per cent of girls and boys aged between 15 and 19 are either married or in union. Generally, physical and emotional aggressions are recurrent many households.

“My rapists took away my dignity as a woman but they did not destroy my soul”

In CAR’s northern region, Marie, 32, and Nadine, 24, share a husband. Between them, they have five children. In May 2014, four armed men barged into their home, while Marie was feeding her toddler and chatting to Nadine who was six-months pregnant at the time. The armed men raped both women and brutally killed their husband in their presence, then burned the family home before leaving. The women took their children and fled into the bush, staying there for several months before they dared return to their village. While in the bush, they did not have access to basic social services including healthcare, they slept in the open air exposed to mosquitoes and ate wild fruits and vegetables. “My rapists took away my dignity as a woman but they did not destroy my soul. I remain hopefully that peace will soon return in my beloved country and this nightmare will end” said Marie.

Ginette, 24, lives in the Central region of CAR. She too, was raped by four armed men, in front of her husband’s family. After this incident, his family rejected her, she said, with tears in her eyes. “I was very angry; I had lost all my dignity and human integrity. I was humiliated and I thought that all was lost for me. That it was now useless for me to live. After this dreadful incident, I took ill and moved into a tent with my aunt in a site for internally displaced persons. My aunt took me to the hospital for medical assistance but two weeks later, my illness did not improve even though I was taking my medications religiously”.

Ginette was not responding well to medical treatment so her doctor referred her for psychosocial support. “They sent me to a listening center where I received psychosocial support, after which I attended a vocational training. After a few months, I am now empowered and respected within my family and in my community. I feel healed and I have gained back my dignity and integrity” stated Ginette.

A number of aid organizations including COOPI, Mercy Corps, Medicines Sans Frontiers, International Rescue Committee, the United Nations Population Fund (UNFPA) and the United Nations Children Fund (UNICEF) are working to help GBV survivors in CAR. They provide a comprehensive health care package of medical, legal and psychosocial services. Just under one third of health facilities in CAR offer post-rape exposure treatment consisting of emergency contraception or psychosocial support for rape survivors.

The legal advice consists of listening to the survivor and documenting the complaint. But considering that the justice system is not fully functional countrywide, the few cases brought to justice in 2015 are generally not being judged as rape crimes but as indecent assaults.

The kind of assistance survivors can seek, the modalities, role and responsibilities for prevention, coordination structure and data collection rules are all detailed in the standard operating procedures for GBV prevention and response in CAR, adopted in late July 2015. This document was prepared by more than 20 state structures, national NGOs and humanitarian organizations including inter alia the ministry in charge of gender, security, justice and health, COOPI, Mercy Corps, Medicines Sans Frontiers, International Rescue Committee, UNFPA, UNICEF, the High Commissioner for Refugee (UNHCR) and the UN peace-keeping mission (MINUSCA). These entities and organizations are providing an integrated life-saving package to survivors. Ultimate recovery however depends on the individual’s own capacity to recover and to rebuild their live.

Nadine and Marie sought assistance for themselves and their children after their ordeal. They were given counselling, medical attention and food aid, which together, helped them to recover from the trauma. Nadine had a safe delivery in August 2014 and the family is now rebuilding their lives. Both women hope for a better future for their six children.

In 2014, the GBV actors mentioned above managed the cases of 6,700 GBV survivors, across 24 sub-provinces. In the first half of 2015, 3,000 GBV cases have been reported, which does not reflect the real figure as stigma, reprisal, family and community rejection lead to serious under-reporting. The humanitarian community is working on reducing stigma in relation to GBV survivors, towards a multi-level approach in sensitizing service providers on GBV principles, working with law enforcement authorities, community and faith leaders, women and youth groups while also engaging men. This is crucial in helping to create a safe environment for survivors to report cases freely. The perpetrator needs to be ashamed and not the victims.
Aid groups could do more however, if they had more support from donors said Dalmonte. “We urge the donor community to strengthen their support to GBV survivors because immediate assistance within a 72-hour period after a rape incident could save a person’s life.” The GBV sub-cluster is integrated into the Protection Cluster which has only received 27 per cent of the required funding. Some major actors have been obliged to close GBV programs in many regions and overall operations have reduced from 37 to 24 sub-provinces.

But prevention is also key to change behavior, to challenge gender norms, and to reduce stigma at every level of society, she stressed.

 

* The names of persons and localities have been changed for protection reasons, story credit to Mercy Corps and IRC.
