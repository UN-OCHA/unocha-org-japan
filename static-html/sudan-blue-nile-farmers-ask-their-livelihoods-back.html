Sudan: Blue Nile farmers ask for their livelihoods back

31 Dec 2014

2014, Blue Nile State, Sudan: Mohamed Bana stands alongside his crops in Ed Damazine, in Sudan's Blue Nile State. Credit: OCHA
Displaced farmers in Blue Nile State want sustainable assistance.

Mohamed Bana fondly remembers life before his home, Blue Nile State, erupted in violence.

“During the harvest season, our village would come to life,” he says. “We used to work hard in the fields and cultivate various crops and vegetables. I used to have a small flock of sheep and goats.

By the end of the harvest season we used to have cash, and many young men and women would get married.”

But this year, as the rainy season clouds gathered and the temperature rose, farmers across Blue Nile State were unable to work their land. Once again, the crucial cultivation period was disrupted by fighting between Government forces and the Sudan People’s Liberation Movement-North (SPLM-N).

Thousands of families in Blue Nile State have abandoned their homes and livelihoods since May 2014. Many, like Mohamed and his family, have moved to the town of Ed Damazine where they look for construction jobs or opportunities to trade at the local market.

“We find it very difficult to leave behind our farms and settle here,” says Mohamed.

A hand up, not a hand out

Fighting between Government forces and SPLM-N broke out in Blue Nile State in September 2011, with SPLM-N claiming that the Comprehensive Peace Agreement, which led to the secession of South Sudan, did little to address regional grievances.

Fighting continues and is driving deepening food insecurity and protracted displacement in Blue Nile State. But people such as Mohamed are clear: they don’t want “aid”, at least not in its most conventional sense. They don’t want to be dependent on international groups for survival. Instead, they want to resume their previous lives and become self-sufficient. Aid groups, such as the international NGO Practical Action, are doing all they can to help people achieve this goal.

With support from the OCHA-managed Common Humanitarian Fund (CHF), Practical Action is providing displaced farmers, such as Mohamed, with seeds, tools, training and technical support so that they can start growing crops and vegetables on land leased from the Ed Damazine community.

“Thanks to funding from the CHF, we have been able to provide farmers with various seeds including sorghum, millet, groundnuts, okra, cucumber and onion,” said Amal Abd Allah, Practical Action’s Community Capacity-Building Officer. “We have trained 42 farmers from the local community, half of whom are women, on agricultural work and to teach their fellow farmers on the best agricultural practices.”

Reaping the benefits

The seeds being distributed by Practical Action take less time to mature into crops. This allows for an earlier harvest and, in turn, more profitable returns.
Mohamed is a fan.

“The improved seeds are good; it took just 45 days to harvest my sorghum, while other kinds of sorghum seed need over three months to mature,” he says. “The early harvest enabled us to sell our products before other crops arrive in markets and the price goes down.”

The displaced farmers also receive training, as do members of the Ed Damazine community. The idea is to foster and enhance cooperation between the two groups. Even without the influx of displacement, food insecurity and malnutrition were already major problems in Blue Nile State. An estimated 58,000 children under age 5 suffer from global acute malnutrition (GAM), while Blue Nile State’s average GAM rate (excluding Ed Damazine) is 18.5 per cent – well above the World Health Organization’s emergency threshold of 15 per cent.

An uncertain future

Ivo Freijsen, the Head of OCHA Sudan, said: “Civilians continue to face the burden of conflict in Blue Nile. This has left thousands of people without access to basic services, including adequate food production and malnutrition-treatment programmes. Humanitarian organizations struggle to get access and provide assistance to most people in the state. Unfortunately it is often those who are most vulnerable, such as children, who suffer most.”

Too many people, particularly children, endure preventable suffering in Blue Nile State. Innovative ideas are bringing much-needed aid to displaced people, but significant challenges lay ahead in helping those beyond aid organizations’ reach.
