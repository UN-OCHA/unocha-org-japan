Yemen: an economy in free fall

16 Sep 2015

Business is down by half since the conflict escalated, says baker and bread-seller Um Ali in Sana'a's old city. Credit: OCHA/Muayad Khdear
The conflict in Yemen has sent the economy into free fall. We spoke to ordinary Yemenis to find out how their lives had been affected.

As conflict continues to rage in Yemen, the fuel shortages are worsening, sending the economy into free fall. OCHA spoke to Yemenis to hear how the fuel shortages were affecting them.

Um Ali

Seven years ago, Um Ali began baking and selling cornbread at one of the main food markets in the old city of Sana’a to support her husband and ten children. Since the conflict escalated in March, her business has taken a severe hit. “I only sell half the bread that I used to,” she told OCHA. “Only 50 or so people drop by each day – it used to be 100.”
Bread is a staple to the Yemeni diet and is usually eaten at every meal but fewer people can now afford to buy it. The cost of a loaf has risen to 100 Yemeni Rial from 70 YR due to prices rises of the raw materials: in Sana’a the cost of cooking gas has risen by half and wheat flour by one third since the conflict began, according to the World Food Programme.

These price increases are driven mainly by the fuel crisis in Yemen: when conflict escalated, commercial imports were severely cut back and oil refineries ground to a halt. In a country that imports 70 per cent of its fuel needs, these cut backs are crippling almost every sector of the economy as there is not enough fuel available to power water pumping stations, hospital generators, flour mills or factories.

As an emergency measure, humanitarian agencies are providing enough fuel to run water pumps to reach more than five million people and are trucking in water to reach 1.4 million internally displaced people, according to OCHA.
 

Sa’edah Al-Siyani

A few feet from Um Ali, Sa’edah Al-Siyani, a widow, runs a vegetable stand. Sitting among bundles of parsley, onions, mint and lettuce, Sa’edah says she hopes to sell enough before the market closes in the evening so she can provide for her two daughters.

“Before the crisis, I grew my vegetables in my own garden which was much better for my business but I had to stop because there was no water for my garden,” she said.

Fuel shortages have caused water prices to double in the old city since March according to the UN. Sa’edah risks losing money if she cannot sell her vegetables before they rot. 

 

 

Khaled Al-Owami

Khaled Al-Owami, 21, a taxi driver, has been waiting one week to re-fuel his vehicle and he is still nowhere near the front of the line. “It will take me another two or three days to get to the fuel station ahead,” he said.

Khaled has been supporting his family of ten on a severely reduced income and now must stretch his meagre means to support two of his aunts who recently arrived with their nine children having fled airstrikes in their hometown in Hajjah governorate.

 

 

Fuel shortages worsening

An attack on Hudaydah port on 17 August worsened the shortages of fuel and other commercial goods. Very few shipping companies are now willing to do business at Yemen’s ports due to the high risks and steep insurance costs.

Humanitarian agencies are prioritizing fuel deliveries to hospitals and water pumping stations to meet critical needs in the short term. But, over the long term this is not a viable substitute for commercial imports and cannot replace the role of the government and private sector in providing basic services and supplying commodities. If more fuel is not imported, millions of people will remain deprived of basic services: more hospitals and water stations will stop functioning and flour mills will be shut down exacerbating an already severe food insecurity crisis in the country.  

Some 21 million people in Yemen – or 80 per cent of the population - need humanitarian assistance, and 13 million depend on food assistance for their survival.

To date, humanitarian agencies have reached nearly seven million people with some form of assistance. Aid agencies continue to advocate for unimpeded and safe access for humanitarian personnel and supplies to reach people in need, and continue to call on parties to the conflict to ensure humanitarian supplies are not obstructed from reaching Yemen in the first place.
