“I worry about my family whenever I hear aircraft roaring in the sky.”

17 Feb 2016

10 July 2015, Sana'a: Ajmati, 6, gets water from a distribution point in Musaik. In this neighborhood, more than 30,000 people are dependent on water distribution, according to GIZ, the German cooperation agency. Every day, three trucks of 3,000 liters each serve a few distribution points in this area. Credit: OCHA/C.Cans
The toll of conflict on Yemeni civilians. Snapshot of two families.

One year ago Mohammed Yasin Saeed* was able to support his wife Suhailah and their five children through his work as a driver on a Government infrastructure project in Sana’a. The family lived in a modest but comfortable house. But when the violence escalated the project shut down and he lost his job, so was unable to make his rent. “For months, I borrowed from relatives and friends but they stopped giving because I was not able to repay previous debts…. We only had the bare minimum,” Mohammed told OCHA.

Since the Yemen conflict escalated in 2015, over one quarter of businesses have shut down, many due to physical damage by airstrikes and ground fighting, according to the UN. This has had a huge impact on people’s livelihoods, with most Yemenis struggling to cope and provide for their families.

The Saeeds were evicted, returning to Mohammed’s rural home in Alangoush village, in Taizz Governorate. Life there is basic: no electricity or running water, and food is expensive and scarce. Two of Mohammed and Suhailah’s children, Ayman and Akram, still attend school but they have a hard time keeping up with homework as they have no light to work by, and must spend most of their free time searching for water.

“The children and I spend one hour walking to and from a distant water spring because there are too many families trying to get water there,” said Suhailah. However at least she does not have to dodge airstrikes in her nightly water search – an experience that still traumatises her, she said.

Attacks on civilian infrastructure, including water installations, are on the rise - water infrastructure serving at least 900,000 people has been either damaged or destroyed by airstrikes, artillery and rockets. Last week, an airstrike destroyed a water reservoir in Sana’a that used to serve 40,000 people.

Not too far away from the Saeed’s former home in Sana’a lives Abdullah Al Hababi* with his wife Eman and three daughters Lamis (aged 5 year), Saina (14) and Shaima (16). “I consider myself one of the better-off Yemenis as long as I live in the capital city where I still have a job and can access some services,” said Abdullah, a communications officer with an international agency. “My main concerns are how to protect my family and the general consequences of conflict that we are all suffering.”

Between March and December last year, over 1,000 children were injured and over 740 killed in the fighting.

Night and day, Abdullah worries for his daughters. “On many nights, my girls cannot sleep because they fear that nearby targets may be hit since we live near government installations and Houthi families, “ he said. “Every day, I worry about my family whenever I am at work and I hear aircraft roaring in the sky.”

According to statistics obtained from health clinics and hospitals by the World Health Organisation, 6,063 people were killed and 28,753 injured between March 2015 and January 2016. The actual numbers are higher because many casualties are not reported to health facilities.

Last year, Saina and Shaima’s school was damaged in an airstrike, but they moved to another one. Some 1.8 million children have been forced out of school since the conflict escalated in Yemen, bringing the total number of out-of-school children to 3.4 million, according to UNICEF.

Like the families of Mohammed and Abdullah, 82 per cent of Yemenis today need some form of humanitarian assistance to get by. One in two people in Yemen cannot find enough food to eat. Today aid agencies are launching the new humanitarian response plan, which calls for US$1.8 billion to help 13.6 million people. Last year, they received 56 per cent of their funding needs but it was not enough. “I am grateful for the generous donor support we received in 2015,” said Jamie McGoldrick, the Humanitarian Coordinator in Yemen. But more support is needed. “I urge donors to continue supporting the Yemeni people, and once again, rapidly provide the necessary funds so we can implement essential activities to save lives and protect civilians in Yemen.”

*Names changed to protect identity.
