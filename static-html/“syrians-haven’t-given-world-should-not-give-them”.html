“Syrians haven’t given up. The world should not give up on them.”

20 May 2015

Al-Riad shelter, Aleppo, Syria, February 2015. Credit: OCHA/Josephine Guerrero.
Interview with Yacoub El Hillo, Humanitarian/Resident Coordinator and Resident Representative for UNDP for the Syrian Arab Republic.

Interview with Yacoub El Hillo, Humanitarian/Resident Coordinator and Resident Representative for UNDP for the Syrian Arab Republic. 

Violence and suffering in Syria continue to escalate, with 12 million people currently displaced, nearly 440,000 besieged and more than 200,000 people killed since the onset of violence five years ago. Humanitarian/Resident Coordinator, Yacoub El Hillo, who has led the UN’s humanitarian response in Syria since 2013, sounds the alarm on the impact of the violence on civilians and on the country’s institutions, and calls on UN Member States to step up to the plate and find a political solution. 

In a crisis marked by such unprecedented levels of need, what would you say are the current humanitarian priorities?

The top three priorities are protecting civilians, accessing besieged populations and getting the crisis back on the international radar.

Firstly: protection. This is such a vicious conflict… There is no safe place in Syria anymore: being in the wrong place at the wrong time will kill you.

Secondly, people find themselves besieged in areas by all parties to the conflict both by Government forces, by opposition groups and listed terrorist groups. When we talk of besiegement as a tactic of war, it is wrong – it denies civilians confined to these places access to life-saving assistance.

Thirdly, the humanitarian crisis [in Syria] grows by the day and is now the most complex situation the world faces. Yet it is slipping off the radar screen.  We launched the Syria Response Plan in December 2014 and five months later we have raised only 17 per cent of that money. Delivering assistance saves lives, but it won’t solve the problem: Syria is a political problem and must be solved politically. But at least the humanitarian community could be given the resources to do even part of their job. We are realistic: we know Syria is not the only [crisis] situation in the world. Yemen, Burundi, South Sudan, Sudan – actors in all of these situations will go to the same 20 or so traditional humanitarian donors. I believe the time has come for an overhaul of the humanitarian funding system to make it much more fit to cope with the realities of today.  We need to engage non-traditional donors – including Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Bahrain and the Sultanate of Oman. There is much more that we can do to give them an opportunity to be part of the annual campaign.”

Syria is a humanitarian catastrophe, when you look at the speed in which it is unfolding, its scale, and its length… Syrians were living in a country that was taking off economically and developmentally – it was poised to be one of the top five countries in the Arab world to meet the 2015 Millennium Development Goal targets but its development has been thrown back 40 years. While Syrians were once self-reliant, now four out of five Syrians are poor, earning less than US$2 a day… Syria was once the world’s third largest refugee-hosting country after Pakistan and Iran. Now Syrians are the number one nationality in the world seeking asylum. For someone who holds Syria dear, it pains you to see.

Since a series of Security Council Resolutions were passed in 2014 securing cross-border access, to what degree has access improved?

There has been an improvement [as a result of] the UN’s ability to carry out cross-border operations from southern Turkey and Jordan. This has helped us move forward and adjust downward the number of places we categorize as hard-to-reach. (The UN is also authorized to carry out cross-border operations from Iraq but that hasn’t happened yet because of insecurity.)  When it comes to trying to reach people across conflict lines from within the country [known as cross-line efforts], it’s been more difficult. Whatever cross-line efforts we have been able to realize have come down to quiet advocacy, chiefly with government and with opposition groups. And we have a long way to go.

Syria is one of the countries with the highest numbers of security incidents involving humanitarian staff. How does this impact on access and the ability of aid groups to deliver assistance?

It is very messy… It is an exhausting task to map out the actors on the ground and to be able to liaise with them so that we can deliver aid. We work with the consent of the Government and with Syrian institutions that provide services to people – electricity, water, education. We have seen many painful lessons from other countries where the destruction of institutions has weakened a Government’s ability to serve its people. We are trying to augment the capacity of these institutions to continue to deliver services. We are the junior player – the Syrians shoulder the brunt of the responsibility.

What steps can be taken to ensure International Humanitarian Law (IHL) is given more respect by parties to conflict in Syria?

Ensuring respect for IHL takes efforts from all of us. We need a more focused Security Council approach: we have a divided Council that creates paralysis. This does not augur well for instilling an environment to protect human life. It [finding solutions] is a Member State responsibility and it’s ultimately a Security Council responsibility. There is a war in Syria, but that can’t be an excuse for a culture of impunity to be condoned or tolerated. This goes beyond the ability of agencies operating on the ground. A humanitarian appeal of the size we launched is unprecedented, but that is the price of political failure. And the appeal will be even higher next year.

To balance this grim picture, are there any glimmers of hope in Syria?

The glimmer of hope is the Syrians. Eighteen million Syrians wake up every morning and they don’t give up. Syrians are in love with their country… They haven’t given up. The world should not give up on them.

The High Commissioner for Human Rights has called for the Syria situation to be referred to the International Criminal Court. What is your view on this?

It is the High Commissioner for Human Rights who is best placed, among others, to call for parties to conflict to respect the provisions of International Humanitarian Law, International Human Rights Law and International Refugee Law. To do otherwise would be to build a case for lack of accountability.
